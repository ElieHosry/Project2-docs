How to read these documents

This set describes a two-milestone project module: joint teams — 6 CS students plus 1–2 Design and 0–2 Art students (see the Team Formation Guide) — build a real-time C++ application in a category of their choice, with 6 CS goals per milestone (one per CS student) and Design/Art goals from their track pools (Design, Art), plus one shared TEAM goal (Audio) per milestone — the UI commons (TU1 ⭐) live in the Design track. The documents answer, in order: what is the module? → what could my team build? → what exactly would each of us work on? — read them in that order the first time.

1. Start with the Introduction. The module in one page: target platforms, core tech requirements, allowed libraries (low-level only — no engines), the 9 project categories, the trimester timeline (proposal wk4 → foundation sprint → M1 wk9 → M2 wk13), and how milestones and goals work. Everything else elaborates on this page.

2. Keep the Terminology Guide at hand. All abbreviations used everywhere else — T1/T2 (trimesters), M1/M2 (milestones), G1–G3 ⭐ (the common goals every team has) vs G4–G6 (the customizable ones), #N (a goal's number in its category pool), backend scope levels — plus the meaning of the symbols (✅ 🔶 🔵 ⏭️ ❌) and colored boxes. Skim it once; return whenever a symbol is unclear.

3. Browse the Project Examples per Category — this is where teams choose a direction. Categories are listed by suitability ranking. Each category page shows 5 worked example projects with real product references, a requirements matrix (how each core tech requirement applies to each example, including the category's purple requirement), platform/front-end/back-end boxes, and — under "Goals per Example" — a click-to-expand goal breakdown per example. This answers: if we build X, what will it actually demand from us?

4. Compose the team with the Team Formation Guide. Hard rules (6 CS + 1–2 Design + 0–2 Art, max 9), composition patterns, and per-category guidance — the direction you chose in step 3 determines who gets the optional slots ("the bigger surface gets the second slot").

5. Split the goals — every track has its pool. CS students pick from the CS Goals Pool (16+ candidate goals per milestone per category; G1–G3 ⭐ fixed, 3 picked; expandable Tech & dependencies blocks show what each goal needs and feeds — use them to divide work without dependency bottlenecks). Design students use the Design Goals Pool and Art students the Art Goals Pool (lighter, deliverable-sized goals — up to 3 per designer, 2 per artist, per milestone — with quality ladders; the Category adaptations sections translate every goal to your category; the Design commons include the UI goals TU1 ⭐, graded by the UI instructor). And every team, regardless of composition, carries the shared TEAM goal per milestone — Audio (TA1 ⭐) — graded for the whole team.

6. Consult the Appendices when a specific question arises: what counts as a "real" backend (A), what the colored boxes mean (B), how big a goal is and how the schedule works (C), how suitable each category is and why (D), Windows-specific development notes (E), and what the platform options imply (F).

Tip: the expandable sections (▸) throughout these pages are collapsed on purpose — first skim titles for the big picture, then expand only what concerns you.
